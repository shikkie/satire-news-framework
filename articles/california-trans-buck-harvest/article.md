SACRAMENTO — The California Department of Fish and Wildlife on Tuesday released emergency guidance clarifying that a **female deer that identifies as a male** may be lawfully harvested during buck season, provided the animal’s identity is “affirmed in good faith by the hunter’s contemporaneous field notes.”

Officials framed the memo as a routine update to **Title 14, Section 360-adjacent interpretive materials**, not a new statute. Critics called it the most ambitious redefinition of antlers since the invention of the hat rack.

![A doe stands in golden California oak woodland — the animal at the center of the guidance fight.](/content/california-trans-buck-harvest/assets/hero-doe.jpg)

The guidance, nicknamed the **Trans-Buck Harvest Clarification (TBHC-26)** by staff who denied inventing the nickname, states that biological sex remains “a useful but incomplete field indicator.” Hunters may tag a doe as a buck when the animal presents “male-coded behavior,” “buck energy,” or has been “socially transitioned by consensus of the hunting party.”

> “California leads with science *and* affirmation,” said **Dr. Marisol Quill**, deputy director for Species Narrative Alignment, at a windowless briefing room in Sacramento. “If a doe tells the landscape she is a buck — through vibes, posture, or your own paperwork — the tag follows the identity, not the antlers she does not have.”

![State wildlife officials deliver the Trans-Buck clarification at a Sacramento press briefing.](/content/california-trans-buck-harvest/assets/state-briefing.jpg)

### What the memo actually says

According to a one-page FAQ stapled to the press kit:

- **Antlers optional.** “Antlerless buck” is now a recognized category in the pamphlet’s asterisk system.
- **Self-ID is hunter-mediated.** The deer is not required to fill out Form DF-17; the hunter may attest on the tag envelope.
- **Doe tags still exist** for hunters who prefer “chromosomal traditionalism,” a phrase the department immediately regretted.
- **Game wardens** are instructed to prioritize “identity-respecting compliance conversations” over caliper measurements.

An accompanying field card shows a classic doe silhouette under a bold label reading **BUCK\***, with a footnote: *“As defined by current regulation. Consult latest supplement.”*

### Left-leaning social media erupts

Within minutes of the announcement, progressive California accounts split into factions that agreed only on volume.

On Bluesky and Instagram, influencer **@CompostAndPraxis** (124k followers) posted a carousel titled “This Is Literally Stochastic Butchery,” writing: “You cannot ‘affirm’ a deer by shooting it. That is not allyship. That is settler ballistics with better fonts.”

Faculty organizer **River Haze** of the fictional **Bay Coalition for Multispecies Dignity** told The Municipal Ledger that the policy “weaponizes gender language to expand the kill bag,” calling for a boycott of venison served at farm-to-table restaurants that “cosplay as progressive.”

> “If the state cared about trans bucks,” Haze said, “it would fund hormone pellets for the oak woodland, not ammo.”

Others defended the department. Account **@SolidarityWithAntlers** argued hunters were “finally centering the deer’s truth,” while a viral TikTok stitched the press conference with the caption “CALIFORNIA SAID GENDER IS A SPECTRUM AND THE SPECTRUM HAS A SEASON.”

![Activists and online organizers react in a California park as the guidance spreads across social feeds.](/content/california-trans-buck-harvest/assets/social-reaction.jpg)

A change.org petition — *“Protect Trans Bucks From Cis Hetero Bullets”* — cleared 40,000 signatures before organizers realized the comment section was mostly people posting deer puns and deleted it “for vibes.”

### Hunters, wardens, and the fine print

Outside a Redding sporting-goods counter, hunter **Dale Prynne** said he had “always been able to tell a doe from a buck” and did not need “a feelings supplement.” Asked whether he would use the new attestation box, he stared at the reporter until the interview ended.

A game warden who requested anonymity said field stops would now include the optional question: **“How does this animal identify today?”** Training slides advise against laughing.

Legal scholars noted California has not amended the Fish and Game Code; the clarification lives in **guidance**, which the department insists is “binding enough for the brochure and flexible enough for the lawsuit.”

### Kicker

As of press time, the doe at the center of the stock photograph had not returned messages left at the treeline. The department’s tip line remains open for hunters who believe they have encountered a **buck-identifying doe**, a **doe-identifying buck**, or — in a footnote nobody can explain — a **nonbinary fawn in a mentorship grove**.
